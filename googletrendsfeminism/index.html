The Feminist "Trend"


A Look at Google Searches Through the Last Decade



The last couple years have marked a renewed age for feminism – one that evolved the word from a taboo phrase to one plastered everywhere – from mainstream academia to fashionable sweatshirts. This era, facilitated through the digital age and bolstered by online dialogue, relies not only on what, but also on how, information is circulated online. Often, Google serves as a primary gateway for awareness and education in such circumstances. Thus, this project takes a look at the rise of feminism and accompanying topics, through the lens of Google searches over the last decade. These graphs chart the statistics specific to the United States, in addition to those of the globe at large. The two datasets illustrate similar trends, which is notable in contextualizing the US within the global conversation on feminism. We begin to witness that, perhaps, feminism in the modern age is not so much a temporary trend, rather one consistently upward.
To assess this, we've collected relevant Google Trends data, which assigns a relative normalized popularity score to any given search topic per month. It can be broken down by location – as noted, we stuck with general worldwide data, in addition to U.S. specific information. According to Google, "Trends adjusts search data to make comparisons between terms easier. Each data point is divided by the total searches of the geography and time range it represents, to compare relative popularity. The resulting numbers then get scaled on a range of 0 to 100 based on a topics proportion to all searches." As such, each value is a ratio of the topic's search volume to the sum of total potential search volumes. The scores are most helpful when compared to one another, which is how they're being availed here. We explore five phrases and topics deeply integral to feminism at its core.





I. "feminism" – World and U.S.


Hover over the points to see exact score values.

First, let's take a look at the main word "feminism" itself. It's relative popularity has indeed grown over time, with noted spikes. These months indeed correspond to cultural milestones that generated interest in the topic and thus increase in its searches. To note, when jumps between months are two severe, dots are left off the trend line. That said, we can refresh ourselves on events that may have caused some of these jumps.
October 2014 – Emma Watson speaks to the UN about gender equality, i.e. the most circulated public explanation of feminism in the modern age
January 2017 – the first Women's March, sparked in the United States and later popularized around the world, stands up to Donald Trump's inauguration
March 2017 – International Women's Day, though not a new holiday, has renewed attention following the increased focus on women's rights following the March
October 2017 – the #MeToo movement shatters many social taboo and unleashes a global outcry of women sharing their stories of rape, assault, and harrasment
March 2018 – another spike in March coincides with another International Women's Day in a new social climate, this time in a specifically "post-#MeToo" one 
October 2018 – the US watches Dr. Christine Blasey Ford, who testifies to Congress her sexual assault perpetrated by Supreme Court nominee Brett Kavanaugh

III. "women's rights" – World and U.S.



Hover over the points to see exact score values.

Next up, we analyze the phrase "women' rights." The notable peak, by quite a longshot, swells right at January 2017, exactly during the time of the first Women's March. While the feelings of frustration by many women surrounding Trump's election explain this bump, it is striking that there is not even a close second-place maximum in this graph. It may be due to the fact that "women's rights" as a phrase, though now associated with feminisim, existed in the modern mainstream independently before the movement did. As such, it's plausible that while "feminism" as a term had been warming up to many people over the last ten years, "women's rights" as a phrase entered the last decade as an already nonnegotiable, easily comprehended concept.




IV. "international women's day" – World and U.S.



Hover over the points to see exact score values.

This graph looks a bit different, and that's entirely on point. Mapping the annual International Women's Day, this chart spikes every March in honor of this holiday that falls on the 8th of the month. Originally founded in 1920 by German revolutionary Clara Zetkin while she advocated as part of a socialist working women's rights group, this day became assumed by other women's movements as time went on. From Spain to Iran, American to China, IWD remained referenced around the world for the nearly 100 years before it was robustly repopularized a few years ago. As we can see, there's increasing interest, with the main leap in trend falling between 2015 and 2017.



V. "intersectionality" – World and U.S.



Hover over the points to see exact score values.

Finally, we analyze "intersectionality." It's not an exclusively feminist topic, but it's an increasingly important one. The term refers to inclusive advocacy that approaches and tackles oppressive systems of power as they intersect with each other – be they be in gender, class, race, or other spheres. No woman is free until all are free, and freedom from gendered systems relies on freedom from the other ones.

Even, perhaps especially, for someone who touts deep feminist beliefs, it grows more and more challenging to believe that the trend of feminism is all a good thing. To create such a palatable flavor of a movement that anyone can get behind it, without really doing anything, seems counterintuitive. And yet, we keep optimism that interest is growing, notably interest in topics such as "intersectionality," which keep the movement alive, bolstered, and strong – and that is the trend to hold onto for now.
