The story of abortion in the United States commences far before the oft thought point of Roe v. Wade. When it came to terminating pregnancies, the colonies assumed the British norm of law, which allowed the practice until the period coined "quickening" – the point at which a mother begins to feel a baby kicking, estimated around 15-20 weeks along. This however, was *not* considered "abortion." Only after quickening would pregnancy termination even merit such a distinction.

European settlers were not the only North Americans to regularly practice the end of unwanted or unsafe pregnancies. Native Americans also did so, induced by various natural roots and herbs. Slaves, unfortunately though unsurprisingly, only had access to these resources through the gateway of their owners, who likely would never allow for such an option. After all, they wanted more bodies for physical labor.
