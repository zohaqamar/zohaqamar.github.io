The story of "abortion" in the United States commences far before the oft assumed era marked by Roe v. Wade.

In fact, the thirteen colonies assumed the British norm of law when it came to allowing the termination of pregnancies, which were allowed with little controversy until "quickening" – the point about 15 weeks along, in which a baby begins to kick. Notably, these terminations were not considered "abortions" at the time – only after quickening would pregnancy termination even merit such a distinction.

Any story of abortion in America remains incomplete without a discussion beyond the European settlers. Native Americans, too, ended unsafe or unwanted pregnancies, typically induced by various natural roots and herbs. Women in slavery, unfortunately though unsurprisingly, could only access such resources unthrough the gateway of their owners, who likely would never allow for such an option. They, after all, did not care wanted more bodies for physical labor.
